QUANTUM MECHANICS: FUNDAMENTALS AND PRINCIPLES
================================================

1. INTRODUCTION
---------------
Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes the physical properties
of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. It is the foundation of all quantum
physics including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum
information science.

Classical physics, the physics existing before quantum mechanics, describes many aspects of
nature at an ordinary macroscopic scale, but is not sufficient for describing it at small
(atomic and subatomic) scales. Most theories in classical physics can be derived from quantum
mechanics as an approximation valid at large (macroscopic) scales.

Quantum mechanics differs from classical physics in that energy, momentum, angular momentum,
and other quantities of a bound system are restricted to discrete values (quantization),
objects have characteristics of both particles and waves (wave-particle duality), and there
are limits to how accurately the value of a physical quantity can be predicted prior to its
measurement (the uncertainty principle).

2. THE WAVE-PARTICLE DUALITY
-----------------------------
Wave-particle duality is the concept in quantum mechanics that every particle or quantum entity
may be described as either a particle or a wave. It expresses the inability of classical
concepts to fully describe the behaviour of quantum-scale objects.

The double-slit experiment is the canonical demonstration of wave-particle duality. When light
or electrons are fired through two parallel slits, an interference pattern emerges — behavior
typical of waves. However, when detectors are placed to observe which slit each particle passes
through, the interference pattern disappears and particles behave as classical objects.

This experiment shows that the act of measurement itself affects the quantum system. The
Copenhagen interpretation, proposed by Niels Bohr and Werner Heisenberg, states that quantum
particles do not have definite properties until they are measured. Before measurement, particles
exist in a superposition of all possible states.

Louis de Broglie proposed in 1924 that all matter has a wave nature, and this was confirmed
experimentally for electrons by Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer in 1927. The de Broglie
wavelength of a particle is given by λ = h/p, where h is Planck's constant and p is the
momentum of the particle.

3. THE HEISENBERG UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE
----------------------------------------
The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is a fundamental limit in quantum mechanics that states
it is impossible to simultaneously know, with perfect precision, both the position and momentum
of a subatomic particle. The more precisely the position is determined, the less precisely the
momentum is known, and vice versa.

Mathematically, the uncertainty principle is expressed as:
  Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2

where Δx is the uncertainty in position, Δp is the uncertainty in momentum, and ℏ = h/2π is
the reduced Planck constant (approximately 1.055 × 10^-34 joule-seconds).

This is not a statement about the limitations of our measuring instruments, but a fundamental
property of nature itself. The uncertainty principle arises from the wave nature of quantum
objects — a wave can have a well-defined position or a well-defined wavelength (momentum), but
not both simultaneously.

There is also an energy-time uncertainty relation: ΔE · Δt ≥ ℏ/2, which has important
implications for the lifetime of excited quantum states and for virtual particles in quantum
field theory.

4. SCHRÖDINGER'S EQUATION
--------------------------
The Schrödinger equation is the fundamental equation governing the time evolution of quantum
mechanical systems. It was formulated by Erwin Schrödinger in 1926. The time-dependent
Schrödinger equation is:

  iℏ ∂Ψ/∂t = ĤΨ

where Ψ (psi) is the wave function of the quantum system, ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator
(representing total energy), i is the imaginary unit, and ℏ is the reduced Planck constant.

The wave function Ψ contains all the information about a quantum system. The square of its
absolute value, |Ψ|², gives the probability density for finding the particle at a given
position. This probabilistic interpretation was proposed by Max Born in 1926.

For a particle in a potential V(x), the time-independent Schrödinger equation is:
  -ℏ²/2m · d²Ψ/dx² + V(x)Ψ = EΨ

Solving this equation for various potentials gives rise to quantized energy levels — for
example, the hydrogen atom has energy levels En = -13.6/n² eV, where n is the principal
quantum number (1, 2, 3, ...).

5. QUANTUM SUPERPOSITION AND ENTANGLEMENT
------------------------------------------
Superposition is the ability of a quantum system to be in multiple states simultaneously until
a measurement is made. Schrödinger's cat is the famous thought experiment illustrating this:
a cat in a sealed box is simultaneously alive and dead (in a superposition of both states)
until the box is opened and the cat is observed.

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more particles become linked in such a way
that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the state of the
others, even when separated by large distances. Einstein famously called this "spooky action
at a distance" and believed it indicated a flaw in quantum mechanics.

John Bell formulated Bell's theorem in 1964, which provides a test to distinguish between
quantum entanglement and local hidden variable theories. Experiments by Alain Aspect and
others have confirmed that quantum entanglement is real and that nature truly violates Bell's
inequalities, ruling out local hidden variable theories.

Entanglement is a key resource for quantum computing, quantum cryptography, and quantum
teleportation. In quantum key distribution (QKD), entangled photon pairs can be used to
create cryptographic keys that are theoretically impossible to intercept without detection.

6. QUANTUM NUMBERS AND THE HYDROGEN ATOM
-----------------------------------------
In the quantum mechanical model of the atom, electrons are described by four quantum numbers:
- Principal quantum number (n): determines the energy level; n = 1, 2, 3, ...
- Angular momentum quantum number (l): determines orbital shape; l = 0 to n-1
- Magnetic quantum number (ml): determines orbital orientation; ml = -l to +l
- Spin quantum number (ms): describes electron spin; ms = +1/2 or -1/2

The Pauli exclusion principle states that no two electrons in an atom can have the same set
of four quantum numbers. This is the reason electrons fill different shells and subshells, and
it ultimately explains the structure of the periodic table of elements.

The hydrogen atom has exact analytical solutions for its energy levels: En = -13.6 eV/n².
The ground state (n=1) has energy -13.6 eV. An electron must absorb a photon with exactly
the right energy to jump to a higher level, and it emits a photon of the same energy when
falling back — this explains atomic emission and absorption spectra.

7. APPLICATIONS OF QUANTUM MECHANICS
--------------------------------------
Quantum mechanics underlies virtually all modern technology:
- Transistors and microchips: semiconductor behavior is entirely quantum mechanical
- Lasers: operate on stimulated emission of photons (predicted by Einstein in 1917)
- MRI scanners: use nuclear magnetic resonance, a quantum phenomenon
- Solar cells: photovoltaic effect is quantum mechanical
- Electron microscopes: use the wave nature of electrons for imaging at atomic scale
- Quantum computers: exploit superposition and entanglement for computation
- LED lighting: based on quantum transitions in semiconductor materials

8. INTERPRETATIONS OF QUANTUM MECHANICS
-----------------------------------------
Despite its extraordinary predictive success, quantum mechanics has no universally agreed
interpretation:
- Copenhagen interpretation: measurement collapses the wave function; quantum states are
  not real until observed (Bohr, Heisenberg)
- Many-worlds interpretation: measurement causes the universe to branch; all outcomes occur
  in different branches (Everett, 1957)
- Pilot wave theory: particles always have definite positions guided by a pilot wave
  (de Broglie, Bohm)
- Relational quantum mechanics: quantum states are relative to the observer (Rovelli)

Each interpretation makes the same experimental predictions; they differ in their ontological
claims about what is "really happening."

9. SUMMARY
----------
Quantum mechanics is the most precisely tested theory in physics history. Its predictions
match experimental measurements to extraordinary precision (e.g., the electron's magnetic
moment agrees with theory to 10 decimal places). It describes the microscopic world where
classical intuitions fail and probabilities rule, yet it underpins the macroscopic technology
that defines the modern world.
